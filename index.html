01 — Background

Why run locally?

For a clinician-researcher, local LLM deployment offers a specific and compelling combination of advantages that cloud APIs cannot replicate: patient and research data never transits external servers, usage is unconstrained by subscription tiers or rate limits, and models can be fine-tuned on domain-specific corpora without surrendering that data to a third party.

The open-source landscape has matured substantially. As of March 2026, well-chosen local models reach approximately 80–90% of frontier proprietary performance on everyday tasks. The gap is most pronounced in multi-step reasoning chains and creative synthesis — but for literature review, structured analysis, clinical correspondence, and research coding in R or Python, the delta is often imperceptible in practice.

Scope of this assessment

All models listed here run on a single consumer or prosumer GPU costing under approximately $4,500. Multi-GPU enterprise configurations — required for the full-scale GLM-4.7, DeepSeek V3.2 671B, Kimi K2.5, and Mistral Large 3 — have been deliberately excluded. All models are available via Ollama or LM Studio without complex orchestration.

02 — Infrastructure

Hardware requirements

VRAM is the hard constraint. All figures below assume Q4_K_M quantization, which compresses model weights to 4-bit precision and reduces memory requirements by approximately 75% relative to FP16, with benchmark degradation typically under 3 points on general tasks. Performance degrades significantly when models are forced to offload layers to system RAM — a drop from 40 tokens/sec to 8 tokens/sec has been measured on the same model when only partial VRAM fit is achieved.

07 — Security assessment

Security concerns & potential threats

Running LLMs locally mitigates many cloud-specific risks (data exfiltration, API interception, third-party retention) but introduces a distinct threat surface. Each model carries provenance, supply-chain, and operational risks that vary by origin, license, and architecture. The following assessment evaluates each recommended model individually.

Critical principle

No locally-deployed LLM should ever be exposed directly to the internet, granted filesystem write access beyond its working directory, or permitted to execute arbitrary system commands without sandboxing. All models below assume air-gapped or firewall-isolated deployment.

DeepSeek R1 32B Medium risk

Provenance risk: Developed by DeepSeek AI (China). Model weights are distributed under the MIT license and are fully inspectable. However, the training pipeline, data provenance, and alignment methodology are not fully transparent. There is no independent third-party audit of the training process. For researchers handling sensitive data, the Chinese jurisdiction of origin may trigger institutional compliance review requirements under certain U.S. and EU data governance frameworks.

Supply-chain risk: Weights are distributed via Hugging Face and Ollama registries. Verify SHA-256 checksums against published values on the official DeepSeek GitHub repository before deployment. Tampered weights could introduce subtle biases or data-leaking behaviors that are extremely difficult to detect post-deployment.

Operational risk: The explicit chain-of-thought output, while valuable for auditability, can inadvertently surface memorized training data — including PII fragments, code snippets, or proprietary text from the training corpus. Monitor outputs for unintended disclosures, especially in clinical or legal contexts.

Qwen3 32B / Qwen3 14B / Qwen 3.5 9B Medium risk

Provenance risk: Developed by Alibaba Cloud (China). Licensed under Apache 2.0, the most permissive license in this assessment, and weights are publicly auditable. As with DeepSeek, the training data composition and alignment process are not fully disclosed. Alibaba is subject to Chinese data governance regulations, which may introduce institutional compliance friction for government-funded research in some jurisdictions.

Supply-chain risk: Distributed through Hugging Face and Ollama. The Qwen model family has been widely adopted with a large community of independent reviewers, which provides a degree of distributed verification. Always verify checksums against Alibaba's official model cards. The Apache 2.0 license permits redistribution of modified weights, meaning third-party mirrors could distribute altered versions — download only from primary sources.

Operational risk: Qwen models support tool calling and function execution. If deployed with tool-use capabilities enabled, ensure that callable functions are explicitly whitelisted and sandboxed. Prompt injection attacks targeting tool-calling models are a known and active threat vector.

GPT-OSS 20B Lower risk

Provenance risk: Developed by OpenAI (United States). This is OpenAI's first major open-weight release, and it carries a permissive license. OpenAI has published more detailed documentation of its safety testing and red-teaming process than most open-source competitors, and the model has undergone external safety evaluation. U.S. jurisdiction aligns with most Western institutional compliance frameworks.

Supply-chain risk: Distributed through official OpenAI channels, Hugging Face, and Ollama. The high-profile nature of this release means it is among the most scrutinized open models available. Verify weights against OpenAI's published checksums. The smaller 20B parameter count means the model is easier to analyze and audit than larger alternatives.

Operational risk: The configurable reasoning modes (low/medium/high) introduce a potential risk: in "low" reasoning mode, the model may produce outputs with less self-checking, increasing the likelihood of hallucination or unsafe completions. Use "high" reasoning mode for any output that informs clinical or research decisions. The model's relatively small size (14 GB at Q4) means it can be loaded alongside other processes, increasing the attack surface if the host machine is not properly isolated.

Phi-4 14B / Phi-4-mini 3.8B Lower risk

Provenance risk: Developed by Microsoft Research (United States). MIT-licensed with extensive published documentation of training methodology, data composition, and safety evaluations. Microsoft's responsible AI framework includes red-teaming and bias testing. Among the most transparent training pipelines in this assessment.

Supply-chain risk: Distributed through Microsoft's official Hugging Face organization and Ollama. Strong institutional backing reduces (but does not eliminate) the risk of supply-chain compromise. The MIT license is unambiguous regarding permitted use.

Operational risk: The 16K context window on Phi-4 14B limits the attack surface for context-stuffing attacks. Phi-4-mini's 3.8B parameter count makes it the least capable model in the assessment, and therefore the most susceptible to adversarial prompting — it may be easier to jailbreak than larger models. Do not rely on Phi-4-mini for safety-critical outputs.

Gemma 3 12B Lower risk

Provenance risk: Developed by Google DeepMind (United States). The Gemma Terms of Use are more restrictive than Apache 2.0 or MIT and include provisions around permitted use cases. Google has published detailed model cards, safety evaluations, and bias assessments. Google's jurisdiction and institutional reputation provide a high baseline of trust.

Supply-chain risk: Distributed through Google's official Hugging Face organization and Ollama. The Gemma Terms restrict redistribution of modified weights, which limits the risk of tampered third-party mirrors but also limits community auditing of fine-tuned derivatives.

Operational risk: The 128K context window is large enough to be susceptible to context-window poisoning attacks if ingesting untrusted documents. Sanitize all inputs before processing through Gemma 3 12B, especially when processing PDFs, web content, or email attachments that could contain adversarial prompt injections.

Mitigation checklist

1. Verify SHA-256 checksums for all downloaded model weights against official sources before deployment. 2. Run LLM inference processes in a sandboxed environment (Docker container or dedicated VM) with no outbound network access. 3. Never pipe LLM output directly into system commands, database queries, or file operations without explicit validation. 4. Sanitize all input documents for embedded prompt injection before processing. 5. Maintain a local audit log of all queries and outputs for clinical/research deployments. 6. Review institutional compliance requirements regarding model provenance before deploying Chinese-origin models on government-funded or HIPAA-adjacent workloads.

08 — Scope decisions

Models excluded for individual deployment

The following models were assessed in the full enterprise survey but excluded here on the basis of hardware, infrastructure, or licensing constraints incompatible with individual operation.

GLM-4.7 / GLM-5
Requires 200 GB+ VRAM across multiple H100 GPUs. No realistic path for individual deployment. The full model is excluded; no consumer-accessible distilled variant exists as of this assessment.
Kimi K2.5
1 trillion parameter MoE architecture; multi-GPU requirement. MIT license has a 100M monthly active user commercial threshold, introducing ambiguity for any use beyond strictly personal.
DeepSeek V3.2 (671B full)
Requires 350 GB+ VRAM at 4-bit quantization. The 32B distilled variant (DeepSeek R1 32B) is retained as it is specifically optimized for single-GPU deployment.
GPT-OSS 120B
Requires a single H100 80 GB at minimum — approximately $25,000 in hardware. The 20B variant is retained and is individually deployable on a 24 GB consumer card.
Mistral Large 3 (675B full)
Full MoE model requires multi-GPU infrastructure. Mistral's smaller models (7B) are individually deployable but do not outperform Qwen3 and Phi-4 at equivalent VRAM, and are therefore not listed as primary recommendations.
Llama 3.3 70B
Requires 48 GB+ VRAM for Q4 deployment. Partial CPU offloading reduces speed to impractical levels on 24 GB hardware. Qwen3 32B and DeepSeek R1 32B deliver comparable or superior performance at half the VRAM cost.
09 — Recommendation

Deployment guidance

Scoring methodology update

Recommendations in this section apply a weighted scoring model that prioritizes scientific reasoning (40% weight) and inference speed (30% weight) over everyday fluency (20%) and context length (10%). This reflects the primary use case of a clinician-researcher who needs rapid, accurate analytical outputs for scientific problem-solving. Models that score well on general chat but lag on reasoning or speed are penalized accordingly.

For a 24 GB GPU workstation (RTX 4090 / 3090)

Under the weighted scoring model, GPT-OSS 20B is the primary recommendation for 24 GB deployments. Its combination of strong scientific reasoning (78% AIME, 88% MMLU) and class-leading inference speed (30–50 tok/s) yields the highest weighted score in this tier. The model's 14 GB Q4 footprint leaves 10 GB of VRAM headroom — enough to serve extended 128K context queries or run a secondary lightweight model simultaneously.

Deploy GPT-OSS 20B as the default workhorse model via Ollama, using "high" reasoning mode for analytical tasks and "low" mode for quick lookups and drafting. Supplement with DeepSeek R1 32B when explicit chain-of-thought auditability is required — for instance, when reasoning steps must be documented for peer review or clinical audit trails. Qwen3 32B remains valuable for multilingual tasks, correspondence, and long-form writing where fluency outweighs analytical precision.

For Apple Silicon (M4 Max 64 GB or 128 GB)

GPT-OSS 20B runs exceptionally well on Apple Silicon. At only ~14 GB Q4, the M4 Max 64 GB can load GPT-OSS 20B at full FP16 precision (approximately 40 GB) with substantial headroom — an inference quality tier above what's achievable on a 24 GB NVIDIA card. Metal acceleration on macOS delivers 40–70 tok/s, making this the fastest consumer platform for GPT-OSS 20B. For researchers who also need chain-of-thought auditability, the 64 GB configuration can load both GPT-OSS 20B (FP16) and DeepSeek R1 32B (Q4) simultaneously.

For a 16 GB GPU workstation

GPT-OSS 20B at Q4 quantization (14 GB) fits within 16 GB VRAM with minimal headroom. Under the weighted scoring model, this outperforms Phi-4 14B on speed and matches it on scientific reasoning, making GPT-OSS 20B viable as a primary model even on 16 GB hardware. However, the tight VRAM margin limits concurrent context length. Supplement with Gemma 3 12B when 128K context is required for full-document ingestion.

Domain-specific fine-tuned alternatives

Several medically fine-tuned derivatives of the base models listed here are worth evaluating for clinical applications — notably Meditron (fine-tuned from Llama on medical literature), OpenBioLLM (biomedical domain adaptation), and BioMedLM. These are hosted on Hugging Face and loadable via Ollama. They typically sacrifice some general capability for stronger performance on clinical terminology, guideline recall, and medical reasoning. A separate assessment of these domain-specific variants is available on request.

Summary recommendation

For a clinician-researcher prioritizing scientific reasoning speed and accuracy: a workstation running GPT-OSS 20B via Ollama as the primary model provides the optimal balance of analytical throughput, inference speed, privacy, and individual deployability as of March 2026. Supplement with DeepSeek R1 32B for auditable chain-of-thought tasks. Total hardware investment begins at approximately $1,800 for GPT-OSS 20B on a budget build. All patient and research data remains local. No subscription costs. No rate limits.

10 — Practical examples

Reference hardware builds for GPT-OSS 20B

GPT-OSS 20B's compact footprint (approximately 14 GB at Q4 quantization, 40 GB at FP16) opens a wider range of deployment options than the 32B models. The builds below are optimized specifically for GPT-OSS 20B, leveraging its speed advantage and smaller VRAM requirement to deliver the best possible inference throughput per dollar.

Build A: Single GPU workstation (self-build, most economical)

Primary GPU: NVIDIA RTX 4070 Ti Super 16 GB | CPU: AMD Ryzen 7 7700X | System RAM: 32 GB DDR5-6000 | Storage: 1 TB NVMe SSD | Power: 750W 80+ Gold PSU

GPT-OSS 20B at Q4 quantization (14 GB) fits entirely within 16 GB VRAM with 2 GB of headroom for context management. This is the minimum viable single-GPU configuration. The 4070 Ti Super provides excellent price-to-performance for inference workloads. Because GPT-OSS 20B is smaller than DeepSeek R1 32B, you don't need to reach for a 24 GB card — saving $800–$1,200 over a 4090. Expected token throughput: 30–50 tokens/second. Estimated total cost: $1,800–$2,200.

Build B: Dual GPU workstation (self-build, maximum capability)

GPUs: 2× NVIDIA RTX 3090 24 GB (used) with NVLink bridge | CPU: AMD Ryzen 7 7700X | System RAM: 64 GB DDR5-6000 | Storage: 2 TB NVMe SSD | Power: 1200W 80+ Gold PSU

With 48 GB total VRAM, this configuration can run GPT-OSS 20B at full FP16 precision (40 GB) — the highest quality inference tier, with no quantization loss. This is the only PC configuration under $5,000 that achieves FP16 deployment of a 20B model. The remaining 8 GB headroom allows extended context windows or a secondary lightweight model. For researchers who may later evaluate larger models, 48 GB also accommodates DeepSeek R1 32B at FP16 or 70B-class models at aggressive quantization. Expected token throughput: 35–55 tokens/second (FP16). Estimated total cost: $3,400–$4,000.

Build C: Prebuilt workstation (Alienware Area-51, convenience)

System: Alienware Area-51 with RTX 5090 32 GB GDDR7 | CPU: Intel Core Ultra 9 285K (24-core) | System RAM: Stock 32 GB + aftermarket upgrade to 64 GB | Storage: 2 TB NVMe SSD (stock)

The RTX 5090's 32 GB GDDR7 provides ample headroom for GPT-OSS 20B at Q4 (14 GB used, 18 GB free) or even Q8 quantization for near-lossless inference quality. Blackwell architecture delivers approximately 2× the token throughput of the RTX 4070 Ti Super, making this the fastest single-GPU option available. The prebuilt eliminates assembly risk and includes a full Dell warranty. Expected token throughput: 60–90 tokens/second. Estimated total cost: $5,299 (including RAM upgrade).

The Mac alternative (unified memory)

System: Apple MacBook Pro M4 Max 64 GB | Accelerator: Metal (native, no GPU purchase required) | Storage: 1 TB SSD
